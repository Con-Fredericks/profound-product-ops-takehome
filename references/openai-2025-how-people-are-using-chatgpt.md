# How People Are Using ChatGPT

Published September 15, 2025.

OpenAI released the largest study to date of how people are using ChatGPT, offering a view into how the tool creates economic value through both personal and professional use. The study is a National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) working paper by OpenAI's Economic Research team and Harvard economist David Deming, drawing on a large-scale, privacy-preserving analysis of 1.5 million conversations tracking usage since ChatGPT's launch. Researchers did not read user messages directly; automated tools categorized usage patterns without human review of message content.

## Who's Using It

Usage gaps are closing as adoption broadens. ChatGPT's early gender gap has narrowed dramatically: in January 2024, 37% of users with names classifiable as masculine or feminine had typically feminine names; by July 2025 that share had risen to more than half (52%). Adoption has also grown fastest in low- and middle-income countries, with growth rates in the lowest-income countries over 4x those in the highest-income countries as of May 2025.

## What They're Using It For

Consumer usage is largely about everyday tasks. Three-quarters of conversations focus on practical guidance, seeking information, and writing (writing being the most common work task; coding and self-expression remain niche). Usage breaks into three top-level categories:

- **Asking** (49% of messages): people using ChatGPT as an advisor, not just for task completion. Growing and highly rated.
- **Doing** (40% of usage, including about a third of work use): task-oriented interactions such as drafting text, planning, or programming.
- **Expressing** (11% of usage): personal reflection, exploration, and play; neither asking nor doing.

## How Use Is Evolving

Roughly 30% of consumer usage is work-related and 70% non-work, with both categories growing. A key value driver is decision support, particularly in knowledge-intensive jobs. Usage deepens over time within user cohorts as models improve and new use cases are discovered.

The full working paper (methodology, complete results, and robustness checks) is available via NBER: https://www.nber.org/papers/w34255
